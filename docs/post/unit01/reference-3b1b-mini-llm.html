Core Concept

An LLM is “a sophisticated mathematical function that predicts what word comes next for any piece of text.”

Rather than selecting one word with certainty, it assigns probabilities to all possible next words.

How Chatbots Work: The Script Completion Method

The lesson uses a compelling analogy: imagine a movie script in which a person asks an AI assistant a question, but the assistant’s response is missing. You could complete the dialogue by repeatedly:

  1. Feeding the existing text to the prediction machine
  2. Observing its probability forecast for the next word
  3. Appending that sampled word to the growing script
  4. Repeating iteratively

Example progression:

  • Feed "Can you explain … a semiconductor device …" → model predicts "used" as likely next word
  • Feed "Can you explain … semiconductor device used …" → model predicts "to"

This incremental, autoregressive process mirrors actual chatbot interactions.

Randomness in Generation

Although the model itself is deterministic, outputs vary between runs. This happens intentionally — models occasionally select less-likely words, which makes responses feel more natural. As a result, identical prompts typically yield different responses.

Training: Parameters and Scale

Parameters

LLMs contain “hundreds of billions” of adjustable parameters — continuous numerical values that determine model behavior. No human sets these manually. They begin randomly (producing gibberish) and are refined through training.

Data Scale

Reading the text used to train GPT-3 would require “24/7 reading for over 2,600 years.” Larger models train on vastly more data.

The Backpropagation Process

Training proceeds by:

  1. Taking a training example ranging from “a handful of words” to “thousands”
  2. Passing all but the final word through the model
  3. Comparing the prediction against the true final word
  4. Using backpropagation to adjust parameters: making the correct prediction slightly more likely, incorrect predictions slightly less likely
  5. Repeating across “many, many trillions of examples”

After enough iterations, models generalize — they improve not just on training data but on unseen text.

Computational Scale

At one billion operations per second, training the largest models would require “well over 100,000,000 years” of computation. GPUs make this feasible through massive parallelism.

Pre-training vs. RLHF

Pre-training teaches the model to predict next words from internet text. This creates a capable predictor but not necessarily a useful assistant.

Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) adds critical refinement:

  • Human workers flag unhelpful, unsafe, or problematic predictions
  • These corrections further adjust parameters toward human-preferred outputs
  • This is what transforms a raw language model into a chatbot

Hardware: Why GPUs?

GPUs (graphics processing units) are designed for parallel operations — originally for rendering many pixels simultaneously. This parallelism is exactly what large-scale training and inference require.

Prior to 2017, most language models processed text “one word at a time” and could not fully exploit GPU parallelism.

The Transformer Architecture (2017)

Google researchers introduced the Transformer in 2017. The key innovation: instead of processing text sequentially, transformers “soak it all in at once, in parallel.”

Word Embeddings

Each word is associated with “a long list of numbers” encoding its meaning. These numerical representations are necessary because training works exclusively with continuous values (tensors).

Initial vectors encode only individual word identity — like a lookup table. As vectors flow through the network, they accumulate richer, context-specific meanings.

Attention Mechanism

The attention operation lets token vectors “communicate with one another and refine the meanings they encode based on the context around.”

Example: The numbers representing "bank" might shift based on surrounding words like "river" and "jumped into", encoding the specific meaning “riverbank” rather than “financial institution.”

Attention is the mechanism that allows long-range dependencies to be captured in parallel.

Feed-Forward Neural Networks (MLPs)

Following attention, each vector passes through a small feed-forward network independently (no cross-token communication here). These networks store additional language patterns learned during training, providing extra model capacity.

Information Flow

Data flows repeatedly through alternating attention → feed-forward layers. Each pass enriches the vectors with more contextual information. At the end of the sequence, a final function processes the last vector — updated by all input context and all training knowledge — to produce the next-word probability distribution.

Emergence: Why Understanding LLMs Is Hard

Researchers design the transformer framework. But “specific behavior is an emergent phenomenon based on how those hundreds of billions of parameters are tuned during training.”

This makes understanding why a model makes a particular prediction “incredibly challenging.”

No one explicitly programs the model to know facts, follow instructions, or generate coherent paragraphs — these capabilities emerge from the training process itself. This is why mechanistic interpretability is an active research area.
